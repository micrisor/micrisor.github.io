ACADEMIC BACKGROUND

    I have a PhD in particle physics from the University of Oxford. My thesis, supervised by Prof Alan Barr, focused on the search for new phenomena at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN, in particular those that could explain the origin of the mysterious Dark Matter. Before joining CERN I also worked at the LIGO experiment in Caltech, which famously discovered the existence of graviational waves in early 2016. I now use the data anlysis techniques I learnt during those years to predict how cancer patients respond to therapy.

CURRENT RESEARCH

    As part of the Markowetz lab at Cancer Research UK, my main goal is to predict how each cancer patient is likely to respond to their treatment. To do this we use a combination of data-driven approaches based on machine learning, and domain knowledge from clinicians and biologists. In particular, I am interested in finding out whether medical images such as CT scans can help us to identify biologically distinct areas of a tumour, which may be driving resistance to therapy. We test our computational models of spatial heterogeneity by building 3D-printed moulds that allow us to accurately obtain samples from specific tumour regions.

LINKING SCIENCE & SOCIETY

    Science is much more than an academic discipline: it is the main motor of social development, our most direct and trustworthy way to interact with our world, and perhaps the most inclusive, cross-border and genuinely human activity. Because of that, I spend a lot of my time reaching out of the research bubble, mainly through science policy and science education. I currently run a pan-European healthcare policy programme, and before that I was co-chair of the Science and Education Policy Association in New York. I also teach undergraduate physics, and have run informal learning projects at the Cambridge Science Festival, the New York Hall of Science, or the University of Oxford.
